Behind the Numbers: The UX Process

1. Understanding the User: Stakeholder Interviews

To truly understand the bottleneck, I conducted qualitative interviews with 3 draftsmen (the end-users), 2 senior designers, and the project manager. My goal was to identify pain points, constraints, and opportunities. The research yielded three key findings:

Finding 1: Repetitive Manual Labor: Users were mentally fatigued from redrawing the same standard components for every single store, which also introduced a high risk of human error.

Finding 2: Lack of Standardization: Without a central library, drawings were inconsistent across the team, leading to downstream errors in manufacturing and installation.

Finding 3: Underutilized Tool Capabilities: The team knew SolidWorks could do more, but the existing process didn't support powerful features like templating and automation.

2. Visualizing the Friction: Current-State Workflow Analysis

Based on the interviews, I mapped the "as-is" workflow. This visual artifact was critical for showing stakeholders exactly where and how time was being lost. The process was linear, manual, and had no feedback loops until the very end.

3. The Solution: A System-Level Redesign

The research made it clear that a simple fix wasn't enough; we needed a new system. I facilitated brainstorming sessions that led to a component-based design system within SolidWorks. This shifted the team's effort from repetitive drawing to strategic assembly.
