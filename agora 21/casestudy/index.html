Case Study: Agora 21

Designing a digital platform to bridge the gap between citizens and local government.

View Prototype

The Challenge

Across the country, there's a growing sense of disconnect between people and the governments that serve them. This isn't just about disagreeing with a policy; it's a deeper feeling of powerlessness, a sense that the systems are too complex to understand, and a general erosion of trust. My central question was:

How can we use technology not just to inform, but to genuinely empower people and help rebuild a sense of community and trust at the local level?

The goal was to design a non-profit, public-interest platform that could serve as a digital "town square", a space for constructive dialogue, transparent decision-making, and tangible community action.

The Process: From Theory to Prototype

My approach was grounded in research on political alienation. I identified three core user frustrations that our design needed to address directly:

Powerlessness

"My voice doesn't matter."

Meaninglessness

"This is all too confusing."

Normlessness

"The system feels rigged."

With these principles in mind, I moved through an iterative design and development process, constantly refining the prototype based on anticipated user needs and feedback.

Key Design Decisions & Iterations

1. Clarifying Participatory Budgeting

The Problem: My initial design showed progress bars for budget proposals, but users found them confusing. They assumed it was a crowdfunding goal, not a competition for a limited pool of funds.

The Solution: I added a clear explanation of the rules and, most importantly, introduced a real-time "Funding Zone" tag. This instantly tells users which projects are currently on track to be funded, making the competitive dynamic clear and their vote feel more strategic.

2. Making the Dashboard Action-Oriented

The Problem: The original dashboard was a passive welcome mat with generic links. It didn't guide the user or give them a reason to engage right away.

The Solution: I transformed it into a dynamic starting point. I replaced the static links with a personalized "Action Items" card that surfaces timely tasks, like a voting deadline or a new deliberation. This small change shifts the user's mindset from "What can I do here?" to "Here's what I need to do now."

3. Telling a Story of Impact on the Profile

The Problem: The profile page just showed a few stats. It felt like a scorecard, not a celebration of a user's contributions.

The Solution: To foster a sense of empowerment, I introduced the "My Impact Timeline." Instead of just seeing "3 issues reported," the user sees a story: "August 20th: You reported a broken streetlight (Resolved)." This reframes engagement as a meaningful journey, not just a set of numbers.

Lessons Learned

This project was a constant exercise in balancing functionality with clarity. The most important takeaways were:

In Civic Tech, Trust is the Most Important Feature.

Every design decision had to be weighed against a simple question: "Does this build trust?" Ambiguity is the enemy of trust. For a platform like this to work, its own functions must be transparent. I learned that I couldn't rely on users to intuit how a feature worked; I had to explain it clearly and explicitly.

Design for Empowerment, Not Just Engagement.

The metrics for success on a commercial app (time on site, clicks, shares) are often counterproductive for a civic app. The goal isn't to keep users scrolling; it's to give them the tools and confidence to act in their real-world communities. This meant designing features like the "Impact Timeline" that reinforce a user's sense of agency and celebrate their contributions, however small.

A Living Prototype

Agora 21 is more than a prototype; it's an ongoing exploration. The challenge of civic disconnection is complex, and no single app can solve it. This project represents a humble first step, an attempt to translate research into a tangible tool that can be tested, critiqued, and improved upon.

The journey of iterating on this design has reinforced to me that the process of building civic tools is as important as the final product. It requires a commitment to listening, a willingness to be wrong, and a constant focus on the user's need for clarity, agency, and trust. The work is never truly done, and I will continue to refine Agora 21 based on feedback and a deeper understanding of the communities it aims to serve.
